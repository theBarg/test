While the literary merit of Shade's poem is a matter of debate, these opening lines serve both the poem and the text remarkably well. The very Nabokovian concept of doubling is introduced here with the image of the sky reflected in a window. This doubling will be observed countless times throughout the text, in the relationship between Kinbote and Shade, the relationship between Kinbote and Gradus, the symmetrical nature of both the poem and the novel as a whole, and the marked similarities between Pale Fire and other works from Nabokov's ouevre, to name a few. These relationships will be expanded on throughout the annotations.

These lines are also the first suggestion of life after death, which will also become a major theme later in the poem (and, less explicitly, within Kinbote's commentary). The similarity between the concept of a waxwing simultaneously dying and "living on in the reflected sky" and Cincinnatus C.'s survival and escape at the moment of his execution in Invitation to a Beheading seems important, though whether this is an intentional reference or simply evidence of Nabokov's preoccupation with certain themes is an unanswerable question.

Returning the question of the poem's poetic merit, this opening exhibits Shade's gifts at their most pronounced. While much of his verse is pedestrian in content and awkward in construction, the preponderence of w's in line 1 (was, shadow, waxwing), the internal rhyme of line 3 (was, smudge, fluff), and the generally pleasing sonority of these lines is an excellent example of the poet's mastery of the English language that Kinbote praises so exuberantly elsewhere in the commentary. Does the poetic genius of lines like these in any way justify the publication of the poem as a stand-alone work, complete with kitschy facsimiles of Shade's index cards and a glowing blurb which takes Kinbote's evaluation of Shade's ability at face value? Not even slightly.
